verb conjugation

infinitives: a verbs standard form -- to be or not to be?

When talking about a verb in general we usually express it in its infinitive form

what's the infinitive form? its the verb name preceded by "to." For example

  • to read
  • to write
  • to speak

verbs are conjugated according to their:

  • number,
  • case, and most importantly
  • time

that's a pretty formal definition, so let's try and break it down into something more palletable.

Simply put, verbs explain an action--to run, to hit, to throw--and when that action took place

lets look at an example to get an idea

here we have three tenses in regards to time:

  • past

let's look at some common patterns for verb tenses

you'll notice for past tense the verb to study is conjugated to "I studied"

the 'ed' appended to the end of the verb is a common pattern for past tense conjugation. However, English has many irregular verbs which do not follow a standard pattern. for example the verb to be conjugates to:

  • i am
  • you are
  • he, she, it is
  • we are
  • you are
  • they are
